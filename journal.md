computed w_{k,p} (optimal win probability in the all-heads-wins game) with
mpmath at 80 decimal digits and checked the condition "k ↦ w_{k,p} is
non-increasing up to n_max", which is equivalent to "(B) is optimal for all
rounds when starting from n ≤ n_max coins".

### Updated interpretation for Goal 2 of [vibe.md](vibe.md)

The revised picture is much stronger: **there is no evidence of any p ∈ (0, 1/2)
at which (B) is optimal for all n**. For every p tested, n ↦ w_{n,p}
eventually starts increasing, which means (B) is strictly suboptimal once n
exceeds n_fail(p). The horizon n_fail(p) just diverges as p → 0, which is why
the small-n tables make (B) look perfect for small p.

This is consistent with the conjecture in §10 of
[2406.14700v2.pdf](2406.14700v2.pdf) that for p < 1/2 the optimal strategy
is "infinitely complex". Tentative conjecture to pursue: for every
p ∈ (0, 1/2), (B) is not optimal for all n, and n_fail(p) ~ C·p^(−α) with
α ≈ 2.2.

> **Conjecture (as stated).** For every p there exists an integer N(p) such
> that when m coins remain: if m ≤ N(p) strategy (B) is optimal, and if
> m > N(p) strategy (A) is optimal.

With N(0.45) = 7 the conjecture predicts strategy (A) (i.e. i = 1) for all

**Refined conjecture (compatible with the data so far).** There exists an
integer N(p) such that:

- If m ≤ N(p): strategy (B) is optimal for every k (set aside all heads).
- If m > N(p): the optimal action is still bimodal in k — there is a
  threshold K(m, p) such that the optimal i is 1 for 1 ≤ k ≤ K(m, p) and
  i = k for K(m, p) < k ≤ m.

Equivalently: intermediate values 1 < i < k are never optimal; the choice
is always between "keep m − 1 coins" (A) and "keep m − k coins" (B).

The data supports this refined conjecture for all three tested p; in every
row of the three policy tables the entries are in {1, k, "−"} and for small
enough m the row is purely {1, …, k, "−"} (pure B). The value of N(p) in
our scans: N(0.30) = 43, N(0.40) = 15, N(0.45) = 7. These coincide with
the first k at which w_k ≥ w_{k−1} in the w-sequence.

## 2026-04-16 — Toward an abstract proof that $n\mapsto w_{n,p}$ can have local maxima

### Problem

For the all-heads-wins game with parameter $p\in(0,1)$, let $w_{n,p}$ be
the optimal winning probability with $n$ coins ($w_{0,p}:=1$). Recall the
Bellman recursion
$$
  w_{n,p}=p^n+\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{j}p^{n-j}(1-p)^j\,\max_{j\le m\le n-1}w_{m,p}.
  \tag{$\star$}
$$
Do local maxima of $n\mapsto w_{n,p}$ exist for generic $p$?

### Rigorous results at $p\ge\tfrac12$

1. **$p=\tfrac12$.** By symmetry $w_{n,1/2}=\tfrac12$ for every $n\ge1$,
   so the sequence is constant and has no (strict) local maximum.
2. **$p>\tfrac12$.** $n\mapsto w_{n,p}$ is strictly increasing (Manuscript
   Lemma 0.3); in particular it has no strict local maximum.

Hence the interesting regime is $p\in(0,\tfrac12)$.

### Taylor expansion at $p=\tfrac12$

Write $h:=p-\tfrac12$. At $p=\tfrac12$ every strategy gives value
$\tfrac12$, so $w_{n,\,1/2}=\tfrac12$ and we ask about Taylor coefficients
to the left and right of $p=\tfrac12$. **Caveat.** The function
$p\mapsto w_{n,p}$ is continuous but typically *not* differentiable at
$p=\tfrac12$ once $n\ge3$: it is a two-sided maximum of polynomial pieces,
so there is a corner whose left- and right-derivatives disagree. Write
$$
  w_{n,\,1/2+h}=\tfrac12+\alpha_n^{\text{left}}h+\beta_n^{\text{left}}h^{2}+\cdots
  \qquad(h\uparrow 0)
$$
and analogously $\alpha_n^{\text{right}},\beta_n^{\text{right}},\dots$ for
$h\downarrow 0$.

#### Polynomials (small $n$, valid at $p=\tfrac12$)

At $p=\tfrac12$ every strategy is optimal, so the Bellman recursion under
strategy~B produces the same value as under the fully optimal policy.
Iterating $b_{n}=p^{n}+\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{j}p^{n-j}(1-p)^{j}b_{j}$
with $b_{0}=1$ gives
\begin{align*}
  w_{1}(p) &= p,\\
  w_{2}(p) &= 3p^{2}-2p^{3},\\
  w_{3}(p) &= 13p^{3}-27p^{4}+21p^{5}-6p^{6},\\
  w_{4}(p) &= 75p^{4}-316p^{5}+606p^{6}-664p^{7}+432p^{8}-156p^{9}+24p^{10}.
\end{align*}
Degree of $w_{n}$ grows like $\tbinom{n+1}{2}$; this representation
matches the fully optimal $w_{n}$ on a neighborhood of $p=\tfrac12$ *from
the left* only as far as strategy B remains optimal.

#### Right side ($p>\tfrac12$): Lemma 0.3 applies, $w=a$

For $h>0$, strategy A is optimal by Manuscript Lemma 0.3, so
$w_{n,p}=a_{n,p}$ with $a_{n}=p^{n}+(1-p^{n}-(1-p)^{n})a_{n-1}$,
$a_{0}=1$. The right-derivatives $\alpha_{n}^{\text{right}}$ satisfy the
simple recursion
$$
  \alpha_{n}^{\text{right}}\;=\;\frac{n}{2^{n-1}}+\Bigl(1-\frac{1}{2^{n-1}}\Bigr)\alpha_{n-1}^{\text{right}},\qquad\alpha_{1}^{\text{right}}=1.
$$
They are strictly increasing in $n$ (as they must be by Lemma 0.3):
$1,\;\tfrac32,\;\tfrac{15}{8},\;\tfrac{137}{64},\;\tfrac{2375}{1024},\dots\,\to\,
\alpha_{\infty}^{\text{right}}\approx 2.607$.

#### Left side ($p<\tfrac12$): recursion involves a "min"

Writing $M_{n,j}=\max_{j\le m\le n-1}w_{m,p}$, for $h\uparrow0$ we have
$M_{n,j}=\tfrac12+\min_{j\le m\le n-1}\alpha_m^{\text{left}}\cdot h+O(h^{2})$
(the $w_{m}$ with *smallest* $\alpha_{m}^{\text{left}}$ dominates for
$h<0$). Differentiating the Bellman at $p=\tfrac12$ and using the identity
$\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{j}(n-2j)=0$ gives
$$
  \alpha_{n}^{\text{left}}\;=\;\frac{n}{2^{n-1}}+\frac{1}{2^{n}}\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{j}\,\min_{j\le m\le n-1}\alpha_{m}^{\text{left}},\qquad\alpha_{1}^{\text{left}}=1.
$$

Numerically (to 18 digits, using mpmath):
$$
\begin{array}{c|l}
  n & \alpha_{n}^{\text{left}}\\\hline
  1 & 1.000000000000000000\\
  2 & 1.500000000000000000\\
  3 & 1.687500000000000000\\
  4 & 1.734375000000000000\\
  5 & 1.735839843750000000\\
  6 & 1.729385375976562500\\
  7 & 1.722763895988464355\\
  8 & 1.717139652930200100\\
  9 & 1.712851968293762180\\
  10& 1.709751579242984930\\
  \vdots & \vdots\\
  \infty & \approx 1.703
\end{array}
$$

**Observation.** $\alpha_{n}^{\text{left}}$ is *not monotone*: it strictly
increases for $n=1,\dots,5$ and strictly decreases for $n\ge5$, converging
to a limit $\approx 1.703$.

#### Consequence at first order in $h$ for $p<\tfrac12$

For $h<0$ small,
$w_{n,p}-w_{n-1,p}=(\alpha_{n}^{\text{left}}-\alpha_{n-1}^{\text{left}})\,h+O(h^{2})$.
The sign of this difference is opposite to the sign of
$\Delta\alpha_{n}:=\alpha_{n}^{\text{left}}-\alpha_{n-1}^{\text{left}}$:

* $\Delta\alpha_{n}>0$ for $n=2,\dots,5$, so $w_{n,p}<w_{n-1,p}$ for these $n$.
* $\Delta\alpha_{n}<0$ for $n\ge 6$, so $w_{n,p}>w_{n-1,p}$ for these $n$.

Hence at first order in $h<0$ the sequence is valley-shaped with **one**
strict local minimum at $n=5$ and **no** local maximum. This is precisely
what the numerical data at $p=0.49$ shows (first monotonicity failure at
$k=6$; no local maximum below $n=152$).

**Correction of an earlier claim in this journal.** A previous version
here asserted that $\alpha_{n}$ is strictly increasing; that was based on
a computation that used strategy-B's polynomial, whose *right*-derivatives
would agree with the erroneous claim. The correct picture is the two-sided
one above: $\alpha^{\text{right}}$ is monotone increasing, while
$\alpha^{\text{left}}$ has its maximum at $n=5$ and then decreases.

$$
The differences $\Delta\beta_{n}=\beta_{n}-\beta_{n-1}$ change sign

can in principle conspire with the first-order valley to produce a local
maximum once $h$ is not too small. This is the perturbative echo of the
purely non-perturbative local-max phenomenon we see numerically away from
$p=\tfrac12$.

### Reduction of "$w$ monotone" to "strategy B optimal"

Let $b_{n,p}$ denote the winning probability under strategy (B).
Recursively $b_{n,p}=p^{n}+\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{j}p^{n-j}(1-p)^{j}b_{j,p}$
with $b_{0,p}:=1$.

**Lemma.** If $(w_{m,p})_{m=1}^{N}$ is monotone non-increasing, then
$w_{m,p}=b_{m,p}$ for every $m\in\{1,\dots,N\}$.

*Proof.* Induction. For $m=1$ both sides equal $p$. For $2\le m\le N$, the
hypothesis gives $\max_{j\le m'\le m-1}w_{m',p}=w_{j,p}=b_{j,p}$ by the
inductive hypothesis, so $(\star)$ reduces exactly to the recursion for
$b_{m,p}$. $\square$

**Consequence.** Any candidate counter-example to "$w$ has a local max" in
which $(w_n)$ is monotone non-increasing must satisfy $w_n=b_n$ for all
$n$. In that case, local maxima of $w$ coincide with local maxima of $b$.
Thus *it suffices to show that $n\mapsto b_{n,p}$ is not monotone
non-increasing*.

### Small-$p$ heuristic

Substituting $(1-p)^k\approx1-kp$ for small $p$ into $(\star)$ (with
$w=b$) gives $b_{n,p}\approx n!\,p^{n}$ to leading order, so
$$
  \frac{b_{n,p}}{b_{n-1,p}}\;\approx\;n\,p.
$$
This ratio exceeds $1$ for $n>1/p$ and is below $1$ for $n<1/p$. At
leading order, therefore, $b_n$ is *increasing* until $n\approx1/p$ and
decreasing thereafter, producing a local maximum near $n\approx1/p$. The
heuristic matches our scan qualitatively (e.g.\ at $p=0.08$ the first
monotonicity failure was at $k=1148$, while $1/p=12.5$ — so the regime
above is only the tail end of a longer oscillation; the very first peak
of $b$ is indeed at small $n$, but the *first* peak of $w$ is pushed out
by the fact that $w$ aggregates the peaks in the Bellman $\max$).

### Rigorous existence at a specific $p$

At $p=21/50$ the sequence $(w_{m,p})_{m=1}^{10}$ computed by iterating
$(\star)$ in exact rational arithmetic satisfies
$$
  w_{9,p}>w_{8,p}\qquad\text{and}\qquad w_{9,p}>w_{10,p},
$$
so $w_9$ is a strict local maximum. A fully explicit proof via reduced
rational differences is in
[Manuscript/proof\_intermediate\_p042.tex](Manuscript/proof_intermediate_p042.tex).

### Where this leaves us

- The local-maximum phenomenon is *non-perturbative* near $p=\tfrac12$;
  Taylor expansion at $p=\tfrac12$ gives no local maxima at any finite
  order for $p<\tfrac12$.
- **If** $b_{n,p}$ is not monotone non-increasing, *then* $w$ has a local
  maximum (via the reduction lemma plus the manuscript's lemma stating
  $w_{n-1}=\max_{k\le n-1}w_k\Rightarrow w_n<w_{n-1}$, which prevents $w$
  from growing forever). The small-$p$ heuristic and the $p=21/50$
  calculation support this, but a **general proof that
  $n\mapsto b_{n,p}$ is not monotone non-increasing for every
  $p\in(0,\tfrac12)$ is still missing**.

### Conjecture (open)

For every $p\in(0,\tfrac12)$, the sequence $(w_{n,p})_{n\ge1}$ has
infinitely many strict local maxima.

This is compatible with the "infinitely complex optimal strategy"
conjecture in §10 of [2406.14700v2.pdf](2406.14700v2.pdf).

## 2026-04-17 — Local-maximum condition (TeX) and MDP-theory references

### Local-maximum condition

The Bellman equation $(\star)$ for $w_{n,p}$ implies that, *if*
$w_{n^\star,p}$ is the running maximum of $(w_{m,p})_{m=1}^{n^\star}$,
then
\[
  w_{n^\star,p}>w_{n^\star+1,p}
  \;\Longleftrightarrow\;
  w_{n^\star,p}\;>\;\frac{p^{\,n^\star+1}}{p^{\,n^\star+1}+(1-p)^{\,n^\star+1}}.
\]
Combined with the reduction lemma (cf. previous section), the first
strict local maximum of $n\mapsto w_{n,p}$ coincides with the first
index at which the strategy-(B) sequence $b_{n,p}$ stops being monotone
non-increasing — and the right-hand inequality above is then automatic.
Full derivation, four equivalent forms, and the "first-local-max via
$b$" corollary are in
[Manuscript/localmaximum.tex](Manuscript/localmaximum.tex)
(compiled to [Manuscript/localmaximum.pdf](Manuscript/localmaximum.pdf)).

### Classification of the problem

The coin game is *not* a discounted MDP. It is a **stochastic
shortest-path (SSP) MDP** with terminal reward:

- State: $n$ = coins remaining; absorbing states "win" ($n=0$) and
  "lose" (forced when $k=0$).
- Reward: $0$ on every non-terminal transition, $1$ on absorption into
  "win"; no discount ($\beta=1$).
- Every policy is *proper* — termination probability per round is at
  least $p^{n}+(1-p)^{n}>0$ — so the Bellman operator is a contraction
  in the SSP-weighted sup-norm (Bertsekas–Tsitsiklis 1991), and the
  standard structural tools for discounted/SSP MDPs apply.

### Three structural facts about $b_{n,p}$

Added to [Manuscript/localmaximum.tex](Manuscript/localmaximum.tex)
and verified numerically. Throughout, $q=1-p$.

1. **Binomial-mixture identity.** $(1+q^n)\,b_{n,p}=\mathbb E[b_{J,p}]$,
   $J\sim\mathrm{Bin}(n,q)$. Equivalently,
   $\mathbb E[b_{J,p}-b_{n,p}]=q^n\,b_{n,p}$.
2. **Polynomial structure.** $b_{n,p}=p^n\,P_n(q)$ where $P_n$ is a
   polynomial in $q$ of degree $\binom{n}{2}$ with **non-negative
   integer coefficients**, satisfying $P_n(0)=1$, $P_n(1)=F_n$
   (the $n$-th Fubini number, OEIS A000670), $P_n(1/2)=2^{n-1}$.
   The recursion is
   $P_n(q)=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\binom{n}{j}q^j\,P_j(q)$.
3. **Exponential generating function.** $G(x):=\sum b_{n,p}x^n/n!$
   satisfies $G(x)=1+(e^{px}-1)\,G(qx)=\sum_{k\ge0}\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}(e^{pq^ix}-1)$.

### Forward-difference identity and reduction

Let $\Delta b_n:=b_{n,p}-b_{n-1,p}$. From the binomial-mixture identity
at $n$ and $n-1$, and the coupling
$J_n\stackrel{d}{=}J_{n-1}+\mathrm{Bern}(q)$:
\[
  \Delta b_n
  \;=\;q\,\mathbb E\bigl[\Delta b_{J_{n-1}+1}\bigr]
  \;+\;q^{\,n-1}\bigl(b_{n-1,p}-q\,b_{n,p}\bigr).
\]
Verified exactly (Fraction arithmetic) at $p=21/50$, $n=2,\dots,11$.

Under the contradiction hypothesis "$b$ monotone non-increasing for all
$n\ge1$", the first term is $\le 0$ and the second is
$\ge q^{n-1}(1-q)b_{n-1,p}\ge q^{n-1}pL$ where $L=\lim b_n\in[0,p]$.
Non-monotonicity (= local max of $w$ via the tool) then requires the
binomial-weighted average of past decrements to be strictly smaller
than the explicit positive tail, at some $n$.

### Two failed attempts at closing the argument (2026-04-17)

1. **Summing (eq:forwarddiff) over all $n\ge 2$** telescopes to
   $\sum_{n\ge 2}q^{n-1}(b_{n-1,p}-qb_{n,p})=qp$ on the right, and to
   $p(1+q)-L$ on the left (computed by exchanging summation
   and using $\sum_{m\ge n_0}P(\mathrm{Bin}(m,q)=j-1)=1/q$ for $j\ge 2$,
   $p/q$ for $j=1$; and $\sum_{j\ge 1}|\Delta b_j|=1-L$). The
   monotone-NI inequality gives $p(1+q)-L\ge qp$, i.e.
   $L\le p$, which was already trivial from $b_n\le b_1=p$. **No new
   information.**

2. **Functional equation on the defect series.** Writing
   $D(x):=\sum_{n\ge 0}(b_{n,p}-L)x^n/n!$ and
   $E(x):=D(x)e^{-x}$, the identity $D(x)=1-Le^{qx}+(e^{px}-1)D(qx)$
   yields $E(x)\to 0$ as $x\to\infty$. A naive first-order iteration
   $E(qx)\approx E(x)+Le^{-px}$ \emph{appears} to give
   $E(x)=(1-L)-L\sum_{i\ge 0}e^{-pq^i x}$, with the sum divergent;
   looks like a contradiction. But the iteration discards a
   multiplicative factor $(1-e^{-px})^{-1}$ whose higher-order terms
   exactly absorb the "divergence". **No contradiction.**

### Compatibility of our Bellman with the two reference papers

Compared to the structures studied in
[references/arxiv\_1703.10746.pdf](references/arxiv_1703.10746.pdf)
(Chakravorty–Mahajan, 2017) and
[references/nsf\_par\_10425749.pdf](references/nsf_par_10425749.pdf)
(Krishnamurthy, 2023). Our Bellman $(\star)$ is

- **discrete state** $n\in\mathbb N$ (no continuum, no symmetry $x\to -x$),
- **SSP** (no discount; absorption at $n=0$ wins, $j=n$ loses),
- with **state- and shock-dependent action set**: action is
  $i\in\{1,\dots,K\}$ where $K=n-j$ is the random heads count of
  the current round; equivalently the next state $m\in\{j,\dots,n-1\}$
  depends on the random shock $j$.

**Chakravorty–Mahajan (sufficient conditions for $V$ to be even and
quasi-convex).** Continuous state $X\in\mathbb R$ or $[-a,a]$, absolutely
continuous transitions, even costs/transitions; folding onto $X_{\ge 0}$
plus standard monotone-policy conditions gives quasi-convexity. None of
this applies: our state space has no reflection symmetry, and we want
the *opposite* conclusion (interior local maxima of $w$, i.e.\
$w$ not quasi-convex). **Methodology not portable.**

**Krishnamurthy (interval-dominance generalisation of Topkis).** Finite
state, discounted reward, **fixed action set** $\mathcal A$, gives
sufficient conditions (A1)–(A8) under which $\mu^\ast(x)\uparrow x$. Our
setup violates the framework on three fronts:
the action set is shock-dependent, the problem is SSP not discounted,
and we want to show $w$ has interior local maxima — exactly the failure
mode that monotone-policy theorems rule out.

Diagnostic check of which assumption fails first if we shoehorn our
problem into Krishnamurthy's frame:

|       | Krishnamurthy assumption | Our setting |
| :---: | :----------------------- | :---------- |
| (A1) | $r(x,a)\uparrow x$ | vacuous (zero per-step reward) |
| (A2) | $P_x(a)\le_s P_{x+1}(a)$ (FOSD) | holds (more coins $\Rightarrow$ stochastically larger next state) |
| (A3) | supermodular reward | vacuous |
| (A4) | supermodular tail $\sum_{j\ge l}P_{xj}(a)$ | **fails**: binomial kernel $\binom{n}{j}p^{n-j}q^{j}$ is not supermodular in $(n,a)$ in the relevant tail sense |
| (A6)/(A7) | interval-dominance variant | also fails: the $\max$ over the *suffix* $\{j,\dots,n-1\}$ creates effective $Q$-functions that are not even interval-dominant in $(n, a)$ |

Net effect: our Bellman sits **outside** the standard "nice" classes
for which monotone or quasi-convex value functions are guaranteed.
This is consistent with the empirically observed local maxima, the
bimodal $\{1,k\}$ policy structure breaking down to "neither A nor B"
intermediates, and van Doorn's "infinitely complex" remark
([2406.14700v2.pdf](2406.14700v2.pdf) §10).

**Use for us.** The two papers are **diagnostic**, not prescriptive:
they tell us *why* the standard structural toolkit doesn't immediately
prove or disprove our local-max conjecture. The actual analytic
content needed is the small-$p$ asymptotic of (B)'s recursion (the
Fubini/$P_n(q)$ structure of Section "A tool for finding local
maxima" in [Manuscript/localmaximum.tex](Manuscript/localmaximum.tex)),
not Topkis-style supermodularity.

The numerical observation has a clean inductive proof. **Proposition**
(now in [Manuscript/localmaximum.tex](Manuscript/localmaximum.tex),
Prop.~6): for $p\in(0,\tfrac12)$ and every $n\ge 2$, with $q=1-p$,
$$
  w_{n,p}\;\le\;p-pq\bigl(q^{n-1}-p^{n-1}\bigr)\;<\;p\;=\;w_{1,p},
$$
with equality at $n=2$. *Proof sketch.* Induction. Base $n=2$:
$p-w_{2,p}=p(1-p)(1-2p)=pq(q-p)$. Inductive step: for $j=1$ the
Bellman max is $w_{1,p}=p$ (uniquely, by IH); for $j\ge 2$ the
Bellman max is over indices $\ge 2$, hence $<p$ strictly. Substitute,
collect, identify $\sum_{j=2}^{n-1}\binom{n}{j}p^{n-j}q^{j}=1-p^n-np^{n-1}q-q^n$,
and $w_{n,p}<p+p^nq-pq^n=p-pq(q^{n-1}-p^{n-1})<p$.

### Structural insufficiency of the Manuscript Lemma toolkit

Combined with the Manuscript lemma "for $p<\tfrac12$, if $w_{n-1,p}$
is a running max of $w$ then $w_{n,p}<w_{n-1,p}$"
([Manuscript/20231006_p.tex](Manuscript/20231006_p.tex#L675-L699)), the
proposition above implies that the lemma never fires for $n\ge 2$ in
the regime $p<\tfrac12$. The set of running-maximum indices of
$(w_{n,p})$ is exactly $\{1\}$, so the lemma's conclusion at $n=2$
($w_{2,p}<w_{1,p}$) exhausts its analytic content. In particular:

- The hypothesis~\eqref{eq:runmax-n} of Corollary~5 of
  [Manuscript/localmaximum.tex](Manuscript/localmaximum.tex) is
  **provably never satisfied for any finite $n\ge 2$** when
  $p<\tfrac12$.
- The reduction lemma + Corollary~3 + Manuscript lemma + Corollary~5
  chain therefore **cannot detect any of the empirically observed
  interior local maxima of $w$** for $p<\tfrac12$.

**Conclusion.** Closing the local-maximum existence conjecture for
$p<\tfrac12$ requires a strictly stronger lemma than the Manuscript
running-max lemma — one that gives a strict decrease at \emph{local}
(rather than running) maxima of $w$. The "tool" reformulation via
Strategy (B) is therefore weaker than initially hoped: it detects
non-monotonicity of $w$ via $b$, but cannot upgrade non-monotonicity
to a witnessed strict local maximum without a new ingredient.

### Partial generalization: $w_n<w_3$ for $n\le 12$

Following the proof template of Proposition~6 of
[Manuscript/localmaximum.tex](Manuscript/localmaximum.tex)
($w_n<w_2$ for $n\ge 3$), the natural next step is
**$w_{n,p}<w_{3,p}$ for $n\ge 4$, $p<\tfrac12$**. Numerically true
across all tested $(n,p)$. The same induction template, splitting the
Bellman max into four buckets ($j=1$, $j=2$, $j=3$, $j\ge 4$), gives
the bound
$$
  \bar w_n - w_{3,p}\;=\;p^3 q^2\,(2p-1)\,R_n(p)
$$
with $R_n$ an explicit polynomial of degree $n$, $R_n(0)=13$,
$R_n(\tfrac12)\ge 0$.

**The simple bound holds for $n\le 12$:** $R_n>0$ on the entire
$(0,\tfrac12)$, so $\bar w_n<w_{3,p}$ and hence $w_n<w_{3,p}$
(Proposition~9 of localmaximum.tex). For $n\ge 13$ the polynomial
$R_n$ acquires a single root $p_n\in(0,\tfrac12)$ that drifts
toward $\tfrac12$ as $n$ grows ($p_{13}\approx 0.4978$,
$p_{20}\approx 0.4911$, $p_{24}\approx 0.4900$). On the subinterval
$(p_n,\tfrac12)$ the bound $\bar w_n\le w_{3,p}$ \emph{fails}, even
though the sharper claim $w_n<w_{3,p}$ remains numerically true.

**Why the breakdown.** Unlike $G_n$ in Prop.~6 of localmaximum.tex,
$R_n$ does not satisfy a clean recursion of the form
$R_n - qR_{n-1}=(\text{positive on }(0,\tfrac12))$. The bound
$\bar w_n$ uses $\max=w_3$ for every $j\ge 4$, but the actual maxes
are strictly smaller and the slack accumulates with $n$. Closing the
gap requires a chained induction with finer bounds $w_m\le w_4,
w_5,\dots$, or an entirely different argument.

## 2026-04-18 — The fair-coin case $p=\tfrac12$ and the $\delta$-expansion

### A standalone note `halfp.tex`

Created [Manuscript/halfp.tex](Manuscript/halfp.tex) (compiles to a
6-page PDF) covering the coin game at and near $p=\tfrac12$:

1. **Proposition 1** (proved): $w_{n,1/2}=\tfrac12$ for every $n\ge 1$.
   Moreover, every policy $\pi$ that satisfies $i_\pi(n,n)=n$ (takes
   the immediate win when all $n$ coins land heads) achieves
   $v_n^\pi=\tfrac12$. In particular, both Strategy (A) and (B) achieve
   $\tfrac12$. Strategies that *refuse* the immediate win get
   $\tfrac12-2^{-(n+1)}<\tfrac12$.
2. **Proposition 2** (recursion for the deficit
   $\Delta_{n,p}:=\tfrac12-w_{n,p}$): substituting $w_{m,p}=\tfrac12-\Delta_{m,p}$
   into the Bellman gives
   $$
     \Delta_{n,p}=\frac{(1-p)^{n}-p^{n}}{2}+\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{j}p^{n-j}(1-p)^{j}\,\min_{j\le m\le n-1}\Delta_{m,p}
   $$
   (the $\max$ on $w_m$ becomes a $\min$ on $\Delta_m$, and the
   constant term comes from the $\tfrac12-\tfrac12$ cancellation).
   Sign: $\Delta_{n,p}>0$ for $p<\tfrac12$, $<0$ for $p>\tfrac12$.

### $\delta$-expansion for $p=\tfrac12-\delta\to\tfrac12$

Substituting $p=\tfrac12-\delta$ in the $\Delta$-recursion: the
constant term $(q^n-p^n)/2$ has only odd powers of $\delta$ and
vanishes at $\delta=0$; the binomial weights become
$\binom{n}{j}/2^n+O(\delta)$; the $\min$ vanishes at $\delta=0$ since
all $\Delta_{m,1/2}=0$. So both pieces of the recursion are $O(\delta)$.

Writing $\Delta_{n,p}=c_n\delta+O(\delta^2)$, matching first-order
coefficients gives
$$
  c_n=\frac{n}{2^{n-1}}+\frac{1}{2^n}\sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\binom{n}{j}\,\min_{j\le m\le n-1}c_m,
  \qquad c_1=1.
$$
This matches the $\alpha_n^{\text{left}}$ recursion derived earlier in
the journal's "Taylor expansion at $p=\tfrac12$" section (with sign
convention $\delta=\tfrac12-p$). Numerically:
$c_1=1,\,c_2=3/2,\,c_3=27/16,\,c_4=111/64,\,c_5=3555/2048\approx 1.7358$
(peak), then decreasing to $L:=\lim c_n\approx 1.7034717608\ldots$.

**First-order corollary.** $w_{n,p}=\tfrac12-c_n\delta+O(\delta^2)$, so
the sign of $w_n-w_{n-1}$ is opposite to that of $c_n-c_{n-1}$. Since
$(c_n)$ is valley-shaped (peak at $n=5$), $(w_{n,p})$ has at first
order in $\delta$ a strict local *minimum* at $n=5$ and *no* local
maximum. Local maxima are non-perturbative — consistent with the
empirical first-local-max at $n=152$ for $p=0.49$ (i.e.\ $\delta=0.01$).

### The limit $L=\lim c_n\approx 1.7035$ and its analytic structure

Three results in `halfp.tex`:

- **Lemma (asymptotic collapse of the $\min$, $n\ge 7$):**
  $$\min_{j\le m\le n-1}c_m=\begin{cases}c_j & j\in\{1,2,3\}\\ c_{n-1} & j\in\{4,\dots,n-1\}\end{cases}$$
  Reduces to (L1) $c_m\ge\tfrac{27}{16}$ for $m\ge 4$, and (L2) $(c_n)$
  strictly decreasing for $n\ge 5$. Both proved by induction:
  - (L1) for $m\in\{4,\dots,12\}$: direct algebraic identity
    $c_m\ge\tfrac{27}{16}-\tfrac{3(m^2-15m+36)}{32\cdot 2^m}$
    (the bracket is non-positive on $\{3,\dots,12\}$).
  - (L1) for $m\ge 13$: the deficit
    $\varepsilon_l=c_l-\tfrac{27}{16}$ satisfies the iterative
    inequality $\varepsilon_n=(1-B_n)\varepsilon_{n-1}+(A_n-\tfrac{27}{16}B_n)$;
    starting from $\varepsilon_{12}>\tfrac{1}{50}$ (verified) and using
    $\sum_{k\ge 13}3(k^2-15k+36)/(32\cdot 2^k)<\tfrac{1}{160}$ (from
    $\sum k^2/2^k=6$ minus partial sums), $\varepsilon_n>0$ propagates.
  - (L2): for $n\ge 13$, the recursion reduces to
    $c_n-c_{n-1}=A_n-B_nc_{n-1}<A_n-\tfrac{27}{16}B_n<0$ using (L1).

- **Proposition (linear recursion for $c_n$, $n\ge 7$):**
  $$c_n=A_n+(1-B_n)c_{n-1},\qquad
    A_n:=\frac{n}{2^{n-1}}+\frac{nc_1+\binom{n}{2}c_2+\binom{n}{3}c_3}{2^n},
    \quad B_n:=\frac{2+n+\binom{n}{2}+\binom{n}{3}}{2^n}.$$
  Direct corollary of the asymptotic-collapse lemma.

- **Theorem (existence and explicit formula):** the limit
  $L:=\lim c_n$ exists, and for any $n_0\ge 7$,
  $$L=c_{n_0-1}\!\!\prod_{m\ge n_0}\!\!(1-B_m)+\sum_{k\ge n_0}A_k\!\!\prod_{m>k}\!\!(1-B_m),$$
  with both product and sum converging at geometric rate
  ($A_n,B_n=O(n^3/2^n)$). High-precision numerical value:
  $$L=1.70347176087173673645200305679541476880577572003162\ldots$$

**Algebraic identity used throughout:**
$A_n-\tfrac{27}{16}B_n=-\tfrac{3(n^2-15n+36)}{32\cdot 2^n}$, with
roots $n=3$ and $n=12$. The transition $n=12\to 13$ is the threshold
where the simple lower bound on $c_n$ stops working and the deficit
analysis kicks in.

**No elementary closed form for $L$.** Comparable to
$\alpha_\infty^{\text{right}}\approx 2.607$ (right-derivative limit at
$p=\tfrac12$, also without elementary closed form). The explicit
formula above is the best available representation.

              (fun ⟨m, hm⟩ => IH m

              (by ⟨j, ...⟩  -- Icc is nonempty since j ≤ N
                  )

#### Technical lessons from the formalization

1. **Termination for finset sums.** Lean 4 cannot extract membership
   proofs for the summation variable inside `∑ j ∈ S, f j` when `f`
   makes recursive calls. The workaround is `S.attach`, which threads
   membership proofs through the sum. A `sum_attach` rewrite lemma
   then converts back to the clean non-attach form.

2. **Nonlinear arithmetic.** The identity
   $2^{n+1}\cdot(1/2)^{n+1}=1$ is nonlinear in the exponent. Proved
   via `inv_pow` + `mul_inv_cancel₀`; then `nlinarith` closes goals
   involving products of this identity with other terms. Plain
   `linarith` and `ring` cannot handle variable-exponent products.

3. **`field_simp` pitfalls.** `field_simp` clears denominators but
   introduces $2^{n\cdot 2}$ and $(1/2)^n \cdot 2^n$ terms that
   `ring` cannot simplify (variable exponents). Avoiding `field_simp`
   and manually providing the key product fact $2^{n+1}\cdot
   (1/2)^{n+1}=1$ was cleaner.

4. **Definition vs.\ theorem.** The full Bellman value $w_{n,p}$ with
   `max` was not formalized; only $b_{n,p}$ (strategy B, no `max`).
   At $p=1/2$ these coincide, but formalizing $w$ would require
   `Finset.sup'` inside a well-founded recursion, which is an
   additional layer of complexity deferred to a future round.

### Technical notes from this round

- **`sup'`/`inf'` over attached Icos**: total wrappers
  (`suffMax`, `suffMin`, `suffMinDelta` returning 0 outside the
  domain) sidestep the proof-irrelevance issues that arise when
  passing `Nonempty` witnesses through nested closures.
- **Casting `Nat.choose`**: `decide` does not reduce
  `(Nat.choose n k : ℝ) = m` (real-valued goals can't decide); the
  pattern is `exact_mod_cast (by decide : Nat.choose n k = m)`.
- **`pow_lt_pow_left`** for ordered fields with `0 ≤ a` is
  `pow_lt_pow_left₀` in current mathlib (the unsubscripted name
  exists only for unordered/extended types).
- **`field_simp` inside a `show`**: when clearing denominators in
  the middle of a tactic block where part of the expression
  involves a `set` definition, the cleanest pattern is to do the
  rewrite via `rw [show ... = ... from by ...; field_simp]` rather
  than fighting with the resulting `ring` goal.
